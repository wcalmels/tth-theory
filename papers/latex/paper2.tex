\subsection{The Hard Problem Reconsidered}

The ``hard problem'' of consciousness~\citep{Chalmers1995} asks: given a complete
physical description of the brain, why is there subjective experience?
This question resists functionalist answers: no matter how complex an
information-processing system, one can always coherently ask why \emph{this}
process is accompanied by experience rather than being ``dark'' processing.

Contemporary theories offer partial answers:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW)~\citep{Dehaene2011}:}
  Consciousness arises when information is broadcast from a frontal-parietal
  workspace to multiple cortical areas.
  Predicts presence/absence of conscious access well but does not address
  why broadcasting produces experience.

\item \textbf{Integrated Information Theory (IIT 3.0)~\citep{Oizumi2014}:}
  Consciousness is identical to integrated information $\Phi$, the irreducible
  causal power of a system over itself. Empirically successful~\citep{Casarotto2016}
  but criticized for: (a)~assigning $\Phi>0$ to simple grid networks
  \citep{Aaronson2014}; (b)~lacking physical grounding; (c)~the exclusion postulate.

\item \textbf{Predictive Coding / Active Inference~\citep{Friston2010}:}
  Consciousness relates to free-energy minimization
  $F = \mathrm{KL}[q(z)\|p(z|x)] - \log p(x)$.
  Elegant but does not explain why prediction-error minimization
  is accompanied by phenomenal experience.

\item \textbf{Orch-OR~\citep{Hameroff2014}:}
  Quantum processing in microtubules undergoes gravitational OR generating
  conscious moments. Physically motivated but lacks explicit collapse dynamics.
\end{itemize}

TTH-Hierarchy resolves these by: (1)~grounding $\phiIIT$ in RC spacetime geometry;
(2)~providing explicit collapse dynamics with quantitative timescales;
(3)~explaining why collapse produces experience;
(4)~synthesizing all four theories as aspects of TTH.
